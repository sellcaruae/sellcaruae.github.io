5 Costly Mistakes When Selling Your Car in Dubai – How to Avoid Them

By James Carter | Published April 1, 2026
Reading time: 10 minutes

Overpricing is the most common mistake — it leads to your car sitting on the market while better-priced equivalents sell.

Selling a car in Dubai is straightforward, but mistakes can cost you thousands of dirhams. This guide covers the five most costly mistakes sellers make — and how to avoid each one.

1

Overpricing Your Car

The most common mistake is pricing your car above market value. Sellers often anchor to the original purchase price or what they believe the car is "worth," rather than what the market will actually pay.

What happens: Your car sits on the market for 3–4 weeks without serious offers. You then reduce the price anyway, often ending up below where you would have started with a realistic price from day one. The delay costs you time and momentum.

How to avoid it: Research 5–8 comparable listings on Dubizzle, YallaMotor, and CarSwitch for your exact make, model, year, and mileage. Price your car in the upper third of that range if it is in above-average condition, or in the middle if condition is average. Price is the primary factor that determines how quickly your car sells.

Cost of this mistake: AED 2,000–5,000 in lost value due to delayed sale and eventual price reduction.

2

Not Clearing Outstanding Fines and Salik Charges

Many sellers forget to check for outstanding traffic fines or Salik charges before listing. These are discovered during the RTA transfer process, and buyers will demand a significant discount or walk away entirely.

What happens: You agree on a price with a buyer. At the Tasjeel (RTA office), the buyer discovers AED 2,000–5,000 in undisclosed fines. The buyer either demands a price reduction or cancels the sale. You lose the sale or lose thousands in negotiation.

How to avoid it: Use the RTA Smart App or Dubai Police website to check all outstanding fines 2–3 weeks before listing. Clear every fine before you list. This takes 30 minutes and costs nothing.

Cost of this mistake: AED 2,000–5,000 in forced price reductions or lost sales.

3

Hiding Accident History or Mechanical Issues

Some sellers hide accident history or known mechanical problems, hoping buyers won't discover them. This always backfires. Buyers run insurance claim checks, and mechanical issues are discovered during test drives or at the RTA inspection.

What happens: The buyer discovers the hidden issue during inspection or test drive. Trust is broken. The buyer either walks away or demands a steep discount. You lose the sale or lose thousands.

How to avoid it: Disclose all accident history and known mechanical issues upfront. Frame it honestly: "The car was in a minor accident in 2023 but has been fully repaired and passes all inspections." Transparency builds trust. Buyers who are comfortable with the disclosure will proceed; those who aren't will self-select out early, saving you time.

Cost of this mistake: AED 3,000–8,000 in forced discounts or lost sales.

4

Accepting Unsafe Payment Methods

Always exchange payment in a public, well-lit location. Never accept cash alone or meet in isolated areas.

Accepting cash in private locations or cheques that bounce are common payment scams. Sellers have lost cars and tens of thousands of dirhams to payment fraud.

What happens: You accept a cheque from a buyer, transfer the car ownership at the RTA, and later discover the cheque bounced. The buyer has the car and you have no recourse. Or you accept cash in a private location and are robbed.

How to avoid it: Accept payment only through bank transfer or in-person cash exchange at a public location (bank, police station, busy shopping centre) during daylight hours. For large amounts, ask the buyer to bring a bank employee to verify the cash. Never accept cheques — they can bounce weeks after the sale.

Cost of this mistake: AED 10,000–50,000+ in fraud losses.

5

Not Preparing the Car Before Listing

Organized documents and a well-prepared car signal professionalism and care to buyers.

Listing a dirty, unprepared car with poor photos and no service history documentation leads to fewer enquiries, lower offers, and a longer time on market.

What happens: Your car sits on the market for 4–6 weeks. Enquiries are sparse. Offers that do come in are 10–15% below asking price. You eventually sell at a discount and lose thousands compared to a well-prepared car that sells quickly.

How to avoid it: Spend 2–3 weeks preparing your car before listing. Clean it thoroughly, fix minor issues, gather service documentation, and take good photos in natural light. A well-prepared car sells 30–40% faster and at a 5–10% higher price than an unprepared car.

Cost of this mistake: AED 3,000–8,000 in lost value due to extended time on market and forced discounts.

Summary: The Five Mistakes to Avoid

Mistake Cost How to Avoid
Overpricing AED 2,000–5,000 Research comparable listings and price realistically
Outstanding fines AED 2,000–5,000 Check and clear all fines before listing
Hidden issues AED 3,000–8,000 Disclose all accident history and mechanical issues upfront
Unsafe payment AED 10,000–50,000+ Accept only bank transfer or public cash exchange
Unprepared car AED 3,000–8,000 Spend 2–3 weeks preparing before listing

Costs are estimates based on typical Dubai market scenarios. Actual costs may vary.

Avoiding these five mistakes can save you AED 20,000–80,000 in lost value and fraud risk. The investment in preparation and due diligence pays for itself many times over.
